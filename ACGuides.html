Area Chair (AC) Guidelines

Thank you for agreeing to serve as an Area Chair (AC) for CVPR 2026. This document contains an overview of your responsibilities and some guidelines for how to fulfill your role as an AC.

As an AC, your role is to oversee the review process for a set of papers, making sure that the reviewing process goes smoothly and fairly. ACs are responsible for suggesting appropriate reviewers, ensuring reviews are submitted on time, assessing review quality, securing emergency reviews when necessary, leading the discussion phase, writing clear meta-reviews, and participating in AC triplet meetings to calibrate decisions and recommend papers for special categories (Spotlights, Orals, Awards).

Contact Info

Your Senior Area Chair (SAC) is your first point of contact for guidance. If you encounter a situation that you are unable to resolve with your SAC, please contact the program chairs. Any questions about conflicts of interest or ethics should go directly to the program chairs.

Main Tasks

Preparation & Assignment (Prior to Nov 27)

  • Please ensure that your preferred email address is accurate in your OpenReview profile. We will send most emails from OpenReview (i.e., noreply@openreview.net). Please check your spam folder regularly.
  • In addition to these guidelines, please familiarize yourself with the reviewer guidelines and author guidelines. You will be evaluating reviews against these standards.

Suggest Reviewers (Nov 27 - Dec 5)

  • You will be asked to suggest reviewers for the papers assigned to you. The OpenReview matching algorithm uses these suggestions heavily. Please take this task seriously to ensure high-quality reviews.

Ensure Quality Reviews & Emergency Reviewers (Jan 12 - Jan 22)

  • Check that all reviewers have completed their reviews by Jan 12. Send reminders to late reviewers immediately.
  • Read all submitted reviews to ensure they are high-quality, professional, and comply with CVPR guidelines (e.g., no LLM-generated reviews, no inappropriate tone).
  • If reviews are missing, low quality, or completely uninformative, you must immediately invite emergency reviewers. All papers must have at least 3 high-quality reviews by Jan 22.

Lead AC-Reviewer Discussions (Jan 30 - Feb 5)

  • As soon as the author response (rebuttal) is submitted, you must lead a discussion via OpenReview for each submission.
  • Prompt reviewers to read the rebuttal and engage in the discussion. Ensure active engagement, especially for borderline papers or where reviewers disagree.
  • Guide reviewers to reach a consensus if possible, or clearly understand the points of disagreement.

Consolidation & Triplet Meetings (Feb 6 - Feb 16)

  • Submit your initial AC consolidation reports by Feb 11.
  • Schedule and attend a virtual meeting with your assigned AC Triplet (two other ACs). Discuss borderline papers, calibrate your acceptance thresholds, and review each other's meta-reviews for quality and tone.

Write Final Meta-Reviews (Due Feb 17)

  • Submit a high-quality meta-review for every paper. (See "Writing Excellent Meta-Reviews" below).
  • Recommend technically sound papers with solid experimental validation to the Findings Track, even if their technical novelty is more incremental.

Best Practices

  • Be responsive. Respect deadlines and respond to emails from your SAC, the PCs, and your reviewers promptly.
  • Be proactive. It is your responsibility to ensure your assigned papers receive fair and thorough reviews. Do not wait for deadlines to pass before chasing reviewers.
  • Be kind. Exhibit empathy and understanding when communicating with reviewers and authors.
  • Respect conflicts of interest. If you notice a conflict of interest with a submission, contact the program chairs right away. Do not discuss conflicted submissions with your Triplet.

Writing Excellent Meta-Reviews

The meta-review is the primary justification the authors receive for the decision made on their paper. It must be clear, concise, and definitively explain the outcome based on the reviews, the rebuttal, and the subsequent discussion.

A good meta-review should:

  • Briefly summarize the core contribution of the paper.
  • Clearly state the final recommendation (Accept or Reject).
  • Summarize the main strengths and weaknesses identified by the reviewers.
  • Explicitly mention how the author's rebuttal addressed (or failed to address) the key weaknesses.
  • Provide a clear, synthesized justification for the final decision. If reviewers disagreed, explain why you sided with one argument over the other.
  • Be written in a professional, constructive tone.

A meta-review should NOT:

  • Be a simple copy-paste of reviewer comments.
  • Introduce entirely new major weaknesses that the authors were never given a chance to address in the rebuttal (unless it is a fatal flaw discovered late).
  • Simply count the reviewer scores (e.g., "Two accepts and one reject, so the paper is accepted"). Scores are recommendations, not votes.
  • Be vague about why the decision was made.
  • Ignore the rebuttal. If a rebuttal was provided, it must be acknowledged and factored into the final justification.
  • Authors may not agree with your decision, but they should understand it (i.e., sufficient and clear arguments should be provided for why the decision was made).

Confidentiality

You must keep everything relating to the review process confidential. Do not use ideas, code, or results from submissions in your own work until they become publicly available (e.g., via a technical report or a published paper for ideas/results, via open source for code). Do not talk about or distribute submissions (whether it is the code, or the ideas and results described in them) to anyone without prior approval from the program chairs. Code submitted for reviewing cannot be distributed. If you wish to invite an external reviewer, do so through OpenReview rather than sharing submissions through another channel.
